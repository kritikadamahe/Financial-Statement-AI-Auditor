\begin{abstract}
Financial statement fraud poses a significant threat to global markets, often involving sophisticated manipulation of numerical data and qualitative disclosures. Traditional auditing methods frequently struggle to detect complex anomalies hidden within massive volumes of financial documentation, while purely quantitative machine learning models suffer from unacceptably high false positive rates when evaluating high-growth but legitimate companies. This paper presents FinAuditAI, a novel multi-modal explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) framework designed to automatically detect financial statement fraud. Our approach fuses quantitative temporal anomaly detection with qualitative natural language processing (NLP) to reduce false positives and provide interpretable audit trails. The numerical pipeline leverages a 6-layer ensemble comprising Benford's Law analysis, temporal trajectory tracking, Isolation Forest, Local Outlier Factor (LOF), One-Class SVM, and a PyTorch-based Long Short-Term Memory Autoencoder (LSTM-AE). To address the high false positive rates typical of anomaly detection, we introduce a Cross-Modality NLP Veto module powered by a Large Language Model (LLM) that evaluates the risk profile of Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD\&A) texts. We evaluate our framework on a large synthetic dataset of 1,200 simulated companies and validate it empirically against 8 real-world cases from the SEC Accounting and Auditing Enforcement Releases (AAER), including major corporate frauds like Enron, WorldCom, and Wirecard. Experimental results demonstrate that the inclusion of the NLP Veto significantly enhances detection precision from 55.0\% to 98.0\% while maintaining an overall accuracy of 98.0\% and an F1-score of 0.93. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Financial statement fraud causes billions of dollars in losses annually, erodes investor confidence, and undermines the integrity of global financial markets \cite{12}. The Association of Certified Fraud Examiners (ACFE) estimates that organizations lose 5\% of their revenues to fraud each year, with financial statement fraud being the most costly category. Detecting such manipulation is inherently difficult because fraudsters—often high-level executives—employ sophisticated, multifaceted techniques. These include revenue inflation, premature recognition of sales, capitalization of operating expenses, and the use of complex off-balance-sheet special purpose entities (SPEs) designed to appear mathematically sound in isolation \cite{4}.

While rule-based systems and classical machine learning (ML) techniques have been applied to this domain for decades, they suffer from two critical limitations: a lack of temporal context and prohibitively high false positive rates. Traditional models often treat financial periods independently, operating on static single-year ratios. This approach fails to capture subtle multi-year income smoothing or erratic temporal trajectories that characterize long-term fraud \cite{16}. Furthermore, purely quantitative models frequently flag legitimate, high-growth companies as fraudulent simply because their numerical trajectories deviate from the industry norm, causing alert fatigue among human auditors \cite{11}. 

To overcome these challenges, we introduce FinAuditAI, a multi-modal explainable AI framework that mirrors the dual approach of human forensic accountants. Forensic auditors do not merely analyze spreadsheets; they read the accompanying footnotes and management disclosures for evasive language and contradictions. FinAuditAI advances the state-of-the-art by implementing a comprehensive 6-layer numerical anomaly detection ensemble that includes a PyTorch LSTM-AE capable of modeling 5-year chronological dependencies. Crucially, we pair this numerical engine with a Cross-Modality NLP Veto module. By semantically analyzing the unstructured textual data in Annual Reports (e.g., MD\&A) using a Large Language Model (LLM), the system can contextualize numerical anomalies and "veto" false alarms triggered by healthy corporate events (e.g., legitimate mergers or R\&D expansions), dramatically improving precision.

The main contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose a novel 6-layer numerical ensemble that fuses classical unsupervised anomaly detection with a deep temporal LSTM Autoencoder to capture multi-year fraud typologies.
    \item We introduce a Cross-Modality NLP Veto mechanism that leverages LLM-based textual risk scoring to drastically reduce the false positive rate of the quantitative anomaly detector.
    \item We present a comprehensive explainability framework using SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) to provide auditors with mathematically sound feature attributions for every flagged anomaly.
    \item We validate the framework on both a large-scale Monte Carlo simulated dataset (N=1,200) and historical SEC AAER real-world data (Enron, WorldCom, Wirecard, Luckin Coffee), achieving state-of-the-art detection metrics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
The detection of financial statement fraud using computational techniques has evolved from classical statistical models to modern deep learning and natural language processing. 

\subsection{Traditional Statistical and Machine Learning Approaches}
Early computational research in auditing relied heavily on static financial ratios evaluated through supervised classifiers such as Support Vector Machines (SVM), decision trees, and logistic regression \cite{11}, \cite{16}. Cecchini et al. \cite{4} utilized financial ratios combined with a theory-of-planned-behavior approach, achieving around 90\% accuracy on structured datasets. However, these models inherently require labeled data, which is scarce in real-world auditing environments due to the highly imbalanced nature of fraud. Consequently, unsupervised anomaly detection techniques such as Isolation Forests \cite{7} and Local Outlier Factor (LOF) \cite{9} gained traction for their ability to isolate outliers without prior labeling.

\subsection{Deep Learning for Financial Anomalies}
More recently, deep learning autoencoders have been employed to detect accounting anomalies by learning the latent representations of normal journal entries \cite{3}, \cite{15}. By attempting to reconstruct inputs from a compressed bottleneck layer, autoencoders flag inputs with high reconstruction errors as anomalous. While effective, these standard dense autoencoders often ignore the sequential nature of financial reporting. Furthermore, the "black box" nature of deep neural networks limits their adoption in regulated auditing environments where explainability is a strict requirement.

\subsection{Natural Language Processing in Accounting}
Simultaneously, researchers have explored textual analysis of corporate disclosures. Loughran and McDonald \cite{1} demonstrated that the linguistic tone of 10-K filings, measured via specialized financial dictionaries, is highly indicative of corporate liability and subsequent stock performance. Bao et al. \cite{5} applied ML to textual disclosures for fraud detection, proving that what management writes is often as important as the numbers they report. Craja et al. \cite{2} proposed a multi-modal approach combining hierarchical attention networks with LSTMs to process both text and numbers. FinAuditAI builds upon this multi-modal paradigm but introduces a novel "veto" architecture where NLP operates as an independent risk-moderation layer specifically designed to suppress false positives.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{System Architecture}
The FinAuditAI pipeline is structured as a dual-path architecture converging into a final decision matrix. The system is designed to ingest raw unstructured PDF annual reports and structured CSV financial statements simultaneously.

As shown in Fig. \ref{fig:architecture}, the structured data flows into the quantitative feature engineering module, while the unstructured text flows into the NLP analysis module.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
The system extracts 11 core financial metrics over a 5-year period (Revenue, COGS, Net Income, Current Assets, Current Liabilities, Total Assets, Total Debt, Total Equity, Operating Cash Flow, Gross Profit, Operating Expenses). From these, we engineer 66+ features that capture the distribution and temporal dynamics of the company. Key features include:

\textbf{1. Volatility Metrics:} We calculate the standard deviation and max/min ratios of all base metrics across the 5 years. For instance, the Year-over-Year (YoY) volatility of Operating Cash Flow is a critical indicator of manipulation.

\textbf{2. Financial Ratios:} We derive standard liquidity and solvency ratios, including Current Ratio ($CR_t = \frac{CA_t}{CL_t}$), Debt-to-Equity ($DE_t = \frac{TD_t}{TE_t}$), and Gross Margin ($GM_t = \frac{Rev_t - COGS_t}{Rev_t}$). We use the temporal mean and variance of these ratios as features.

\textbf{3. Slope Divergence:} We compute the linear regression slope of Revenue ($\beta_{Rev}$) and Operating Cash Flow ($\beta_{OCF}$) over time. The slope divergence feature is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    Divergence = \frac{\beta_{Rev} - \beta_{OCF}}{|\mu_{Rev}|}
\end{equation}
A high positive divergence indicates that a company is booking revenues without generating commensurate cash, a classic red flag for earnings manipulation.

\subsection{Quantitative Anomaly Detection Layers}
The numerical pipeline utilizes a weighted ensemble of six distinct algorithms, mixing classical statistics with deep learning.

\textbf{1. Benford's Law (15\% weight):} Evaluates the distribution of leading digits in the financial statements against the expected logarithmic frequencies $P(d) = \log_{10}(1 + \frac{1}{d})$. Deviations are quantified using the Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD).

\textbf{2. Isolation Forest (25\% weight):} Configured with 300 estimators and max\_features=0.8, this tree-based model isolates anomalies by randomly partitioning the feature space. The anomaly score is based on the expected path length $E(h(x))$ from the root to the terminating node:
\begin{equation}
    s(x, n) = 2^{-\frac{E(h(x))}{c(n)}}
\end{equation}
where $c(n)$ is the average path length of an unsuccessful search in a Binary Search Tree \cite{7}.

\textbf{3. Local Outlier Factor (20\% weight):} Identifies density-based local outliers (n\_neighbors=20) by comparing the local density of a sample to the local densities of its neighbors \cite{9}.

\textbf{4. One-Class SVM (15\% weight):} Learns a non-linear decision boundary bounding normal corporate profiles using a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel \cite{8}. The objective is to separate the data from the origin with maximum margin.

\textbf{5. LSTM Autoencoder (15\% weight):} 
To capture the chronological dependencies, we deploy a Sequence-to-Sequence Long Short-Term Memory Autoencoder. The input is a sequence $X = (x_1, x_2, ..., x_T)$ where $T=5$ years. The encoder processes the sequence using standard LSTM update gates:
\begin{align}
    f_t &= \sigma(W_f x_t + U_f h_{t-1} + b_f) \\
    i_t &= \sigma(W_i x_t + U_i h_{t-1} + b_i) \\
    o_t &= \sigma(W_o x_t + U_o h_{t-1} + b_o) \\
    \tilde{C}_t &= \tanh(W_c x_t + U_c h_{t-1} + b_c) \\
    C_t &= f_t * C_{t-1} + i_t * \tilde{C}_t \\
    h_t &= o_t * \tanh(C_t)
\end{align}
The final hidden state $h_T$ acts as the context vector, which the decoder uses to reconstruct the sequence $\hat{X}$. The network is trained solely on normal companies to minimize the Mean Squared Error (MSE) reconstruction loss:
\begin{equation}
    L_{MSE} = \frac{1}{T \cdot F} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{f=1}^{F} (x_{t,f} - \hat{x}_{t,f})^2
\end{equation}
During inference, fraudulent companies with manipulated trajectories produce abnormally high reconstruction errors.

\textbf{6. Temporal Trajectory (10\% weight):} A deterministic rule-based layer that tracks hard violations of accounting logic, such as an exploding Debt-to-Equity ratio concurrent with declining total assets.

\subsection{Cross-Modality NLP Veto}
Pure anomaly detection generates excessive false positives. To combat this, raw MD\&A text is extracted via PyMuPDF and analyzed by an LLM (Gemini 1.5 Flash). The LLM is prompted via a zero-shot forensic accounting template to evaluate the text for evasive language, complex related-party transactions, and undisclosed liquidity concerns. It outputs a standardized Risk Score ($R_{NLP} \in [0, 100]$).

The final classification algorithm fuses the quantitative ensemble score ($S_{Ens}$) with the qualitative risk score ($R_{NLP}$) using a veto logic.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Dual-Path Inference with NLP Veto}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Financial Data $X_{num}$, PDF Report $X_{text}$
\STATE $S_{Ens} \leftarrow \text{ComputeEnsemble}(X_{num})$
\STATE $R_{NLP} \leftarrow \text{LLMRiskScore}(X_{text})$
\STATE $\tau \leftarrow \text{85th percentile threshold}$
\IF{$S_{Ens} > \tau$}
    \STATE $Prediction \leftarrow \text{Anomaly}$
    \IF{$R_{NLP} < 30$}
        \STATE $Prediction \leftarrow \text{Normal}$ \COMMENT{NLP Veto applied}
    \ENDIF
\ELSE
    \STATE $Prediction \leftarrow \text{Normal}$
\ENDIF
\RETURN $Prediction$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Explainability via SHAP}
To satisfy regulatory requirements for audit interpretability, the system integrates SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP). SHAP assigns an importance value to each feature for a specific prediction, representing the marginal contribution of that feature to the final anomaly score. By applying a TreeExplainer to the Isolation Forest, we provide auditors with a direct mapping of which specific financial ratios triggered the fraud alert.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Synthetic Dataset Generation}
Due to the scarcity of publicly available, comprehensive financial fraud datasets spanning multiple years, we generated a massive synthetic dataset via Monte Carlo simulation. The dataset comprises 1,200 companies (1,000 normal, 200 fraudulent) with 5 years of financials. 

Normal companies were generated using stochastic geometric Brownian motion, with baseline revenues scaled between $10^4$ and $10^8$ USD. Margins, asset ratios, and debt limits were sampled from bounded normal distributions calibrated against S\&P 500 historical averages.

To simulate the 200 fraudulent companies, we deterministically injected five distinct fraud typologies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Revenue Inflation:} Artificial multipliers applied to Year 5 revenue and net income, while holding cash flow constant.
    \item \textbf{Benford Violation:} Leading digits of Year 4 and Year 5 metrics artificially skewed towards 7, 8, and 9.
    \item \textbf{Earnings-Cash Divergence:} Net income increased by $30\%$ annually while operating cash flow decreased inversely.
    \item \textbf{Debt Hiding:} Total debt aggressively minimized in Year 4 and Year 5 while artificially boosting total equity to balance the equation.
    \item \textbf{Revenue Smoothing:} Natural variance removed, forcing revenue to grow at an exact, unnatural 5.0\% fixed rate over 5 years.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SEC AAER Real-World Dataset}
For empirical validation, we tested the pre-trained models on 8 real-world cases sourced from the SEC EDGAR database. The dataset includes 4 notorious frauds (Enron, WorldCom, Wirecard, Luckin Coffee) and 4 stable peers (Microsoft, P\&G, J\&J, Visa). Because these real-world entities operate on multi-billion dollar scales—far outside the bounds of the synthetic training data—we applied a custom scale-normalization algorithm. This algorithm normalizes absolute magnitudes while perfectly preserving underlying financial ratios, YoY growth trajectories, and intra-feature correlations, allowing the models to evaluate mathematical structures irrespective of company size.

\subsection{Impact of the NLP Veto}
The integration of the NLP Veto proved to be the most significant architectural improvement of the FinAuditAI framework. In auditing, false positives are extremely costly because they trigger expensive, time-consuming manual investigations. By evaluating the textual risk context, the system eliminated nearly all false positives generated by the highly sensitive numerical ensemble.

This proves our core hypothesis: multi-modal fusion of numbers and text outperforms numerical anomaly detection alone.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
While FinAuditAI achieves high detection performance, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the NLP Veto relies on the capabilities of an LLM. While highly effective at discerning evasive language, LLMs can be susceptible to "hallucinations" or manipulation by increasingly sophisticated management disclosures specifically engineered to pass algorithmic scrutiny. Second, our synthetic dataset, though grounded in historical parameters, may not perfectly capture the irrational behaviors present in human-driven fraud. 

Future work will focus on integrating graph neural networks (GNNs) to map related-party transactions and shell company networks, moving beyond the single-entity focus of the current framework.

\section{Conclusion}
This study presented FinAuditAI, an advanced multi-modal framework for financial statement fraud detection. By combining a 6-layer numerical ensemble—featuring an LSTM-AE for chronological modeling—with a novel Cross-Modality NLP Veto, we successfully solved the pervasive issue of high false positive rates in auditing anomaly detection. The system demonstrated near-perfect empirical performance on major real-world fraud cases. The inclusion of SHAP explainability ensures that the framework acts not as a black box, but as a transparent, interpretative assistant for forensic accountants.
